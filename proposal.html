01 — Problem

Self-intersection in triangle meshes

Standard collision handling is reactive: detect near-overlapping triangles, then push them apart locally. This offers no global guidance. It cannot steer geometry proactively away from contact, guarantee that a shape interpolation stays intersection-free, or distribute gap sizes uniformly when packing objects together.

To address these issues, we leverage two fundamental geometric energies:

  • Shell Energy ($W$): Models the physical elastic cost of stretching and bending, ensuring the object maintains its reference shape.
  • Repulsive Energy ($\Phi$): Provides a long-range potential that acts as a collision shield, preventing self-intersections.

Elastic Shell Energy

The total elastic shell energy $W(x, \tilde{x})$ measures the physical "strain" or cost of deforming a reference mesh $x$ into a new configuration $\tilde{x}$. It is the sum of membrane and bending energies: $$W(x, \tilde{x}) = W_{\text{membrane}}(x, \tilde{x}) + W_{\text{bending}}(x, \tilde{x})$$

The discrete membrane energy accounts for tangential stretching and shearing via a Neo-Hookean model: $$W_{\text{membrane}}(x, \tilde{x}) = \sum_{t \in T} a_t W_{\text{nh}}(I_t^{-1} \tilde{I}_t)$$ where $I_t^{-1} \tilde{I}_t$ is the Cauchy-Green strain tensor and $a_t$ is the triangle area. The discrete bending energy measures the change in dihedral angles across edges: $$W_{\text{bending}}(x, \tilde{x}) = 8 \sum_{e \in E_{\text{int}}} \frac{\ell_e^2}{a_e} (\tan(\theta_e/2) - \tan(\tilde{\theta}_e/2))^2$$ where $\ell_e$ is the edge length, $a_e$ is the associated area, and $\theta_e, \tilde{\theta}_e$ are the dihedral angles.

The tangent-point energy (TPE) is a long-range repulsive potential integrated over all pairs of surface points, weighted so that points close in space but far along the surface repel strongly. TPE diverges to infinity at self-intersection, penalizes bending, and distributes curvature evenly.

For an immersion $f: M \to \mathbb{R}^3$ of a closed surface $M$, the continuous TPE is

$$E_\alpha(f) = \int_M \int_M \frac{|\langle N_f(x),\, f(x) - f(y) \rangle|^\alpha}{|f(x) - f(y)|^{2\alpha}} \, dA_x \, dA_y$$

where $N_f(x)$ is the unit normal at $x$, $dA$ is the area element, and $\alpha > 1$ controls repulsion strength. The numerator measures how much the vector from $y$ to $x$ deviates from the tangent plane at $x$: for nearby points on a smooth region, $\langle N_f(x), f(x) - f(y) \rangle \approx 0$ and the integrand is small. When two distant patches of surface approach each other, this quantity grows and the energy diverges. TPE is finite if and only if $f$ is an embedding (Strzelecki and von der Mosel, 2013).

For a triangle mesh with $n$ faces, the discrete energy approximates the double integral via midpoint quadrature over triangle pairs:

$$E_\alpha(f) \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{|\langle N_i,\, c_i - c_j \rangle|^\alpha}{|c_i - c_j|^{2\alpha}} \, A_i \, A_j$$

where $c_i, N_i, A_i$ are the centroid, normal, and area of triangle $i$. The naive $O(n^2)$ sum is accelerated to $O(n \log n)$ using a Barnes-Hut style BVH that clusters distant triangles together. Optimization uses a fractional Sobolev inner product $H^{s}(M)$ (with $s = \alpha - 1$) as a preconditioner, which dramatically improves convergence compared to the $L^2$ gradient.

Repulsive Surfaces (Yu et al., 2021) develops this full pipeline: discretization, hierarchical acceleration, and Sobolev-preconditioned descent. Repulsive Shells (Sassen et al., 2024) goes further, building TPE into the Riemannian metric of a shape space via a graph manifold.

Graph Manifold Integration

The core contribution of this approach is integrating these energies into a new graph manifold. Instead of simply adding the repulsive energy, which would cause the shape to unnaturally expand to minimize total cost, the framework penalizes the change in repulsive energy alongside the physical strain of the elastic shell.

The squared distance between two geometric configurations $x$ and $y$ in the Repulsive Shell space is defined as: $$D_\Phi^2(x, y) := \hat{W}(x, y) + (\hat{\Phi}(x) - \hat{\Phi}(y))^2$$ where $\hat{W}(x, y)$ is the shell energy and $\hat{\Phi}$ is the repulsive potential. This metric ensures that if points start close together, they can stay close, but they can never cross the "infinite mountains" of self-intersection, guaranteeing a physically realistic and collision-free trajectory.

02 — Goals & Deliverables

What we're making

A C++/CUDA system that takes triangle meshes as input and produces intersection-free optimized or interpolated geometry as output. We evaluate on visual quality (do we reproduce results from the papers?), correctness (does the discrete energy converge under refinement?), and performance (how much does GPU acceleration buy us?).

Baseline
Repulsive Shells shape space. Graph manifold construction over an elastic shell space, with discrete TPE built into the Riemannian metric. Includes the underlying Repulsive Surfaces machinery: hierarchical acceleration, Sobolev-preconditioned descent, constraints, and dynamic remeshing.
CUDA acceleration. GPU-accelerated adaptive TPE evaluation, BVH construction, and multipole interactions. The near-field adaptive subdivision maps well to warp-level parallelism. Neither paper implemented GPU parallelism; both are bottlenecked at 4-8 CPU threads.
Canonical embedding gallery. TPE minimizers for unknotted surfaces of increasing genus, showing the polyhedral symmetries reported by Yu et al.
Intersection-free shape interpolation. Geodesic interpolation on the graph manifold between pairs of meshes, producing collision-free trajectories by construction.
Quantitative validation. Energy convergence under mesh refinement, preconditioner comparison, and GPU speedup curves.
Aspirational
Continuous collision detection during line search. The discrete TPE does not hard-guarantee intersection-free results. Adding CCD as a safety net during optimization would be a practical robustness improvement identified by Yu et al. as future work.
Curvature-adapted remeshing. Concentrate mesh resolution where curvature is high, reducing total face count without sacrificing quality. Flagged by Yu et al. as a way to improve performance.
Adaptive quadrature on the GPU. Repulsive Shells develops adaptive TPE quadrature that prevents collisions on coarse meshes. Porting this to CUDA is nontrivial (variable work per triangle pair) and would be a novel contribution.
Multiple applications demonstrating the framework's versatility, e.g. both shape interpolation and packing, or isotopy visualization for knotted surfaces.
Multigrid preconditioner for dense constraints. The current GMRES-based preconditioner cannot handle per-triangle constraints (e.g., area preservation) without $O(|F|)$ solves. A multigrid approach could unlock this, as noted by Yu et al.
Alternative repulsive potentials. Explore Mobius energy (with the elastic term compensating for its "pull tight" tendency), a continuous/convergent variant of IPC with larger cutoff distances, or the cosine energy of Kusner and Sullivan. Each offers different tradeoffs between computational cost and long-range guidance quality.
Boundary treatment. TPE is only proven to be an infinite barrier for closed surfaces. A rigorous treatment for open surfaces (clothing, sheets, membranes with holes) could add TPE of boundary curves plus a boundary-to-surface interaction term, as discussed by Sassen et al.
Adaptive remeshing during geodesic computation. Mesh quality degrades under large tangential shearing during shape interpolation. Unlike static optimization (where Repulsive Surfaces does remeshing), doing it during geodesic computation requires consistent connectivity across time steps for the path energy, or a formulation more agnostic to connectivity.
Reduced-order shape spaces. Parameterize the shape space by skeletal rig parameters, learned latent codes, or sparse deformation handles instead of raw vertex positions. The graph manifold construction is agnostic to the coordinate representation. This would make collision-free interpolation practical for animation pipelines. Mentioned but not pursued by Sassen et al.
Volumetric coupling. Replace the 2D shell elasticity model with a 3D volumetric FEM model for objects with meaningful thickness. TPE still lives on the boundary surface, but the elastic response comes from the volume. Explicitly mentioned as future work by Sassen et al.
Topology-aware extensions. A hybrid approach where the repulsive shell space handles collision-free deformation, and topological surgery operations handle connectivity changes (tearing, merging, cutting) at prescribed moments, with the repulsive metric ensuring surgery happens cleanly.
WebGPU real-time demo. TPE evaluation and gradient descent in WebGPU compute shaders, mesh visualization in render shaders, all on the GPU with no CPU round-trip. A browser-based interactive tool where you drag a mesh and watch repulsive forces redistribute geometry in real time.

04 — Resources

References and tools

Papers

Repulsive Surfaces · Yu, Brakensiek, Schumacher, Crane (2021)
Primary reference. TPE discretization, fractional preconditioning, hierarchical acceleration.
Repulsive Shells · Sassen, Schumacher, Rumpf, Crane (2024)
Graph manifold construction, shape space geodesics, adaptive TPE quadrature.
Repulsive Curves · Yu, Schumacher, Crane (2021)
Same ideas in the simpler curve setting. Good for building intuition.
Tangent-point repulsive potentials · Strzelecki & von der Mosel (2013)
Mathematical foundations. TPE is finite iff the surface is embedded.
Time-Discrete Geodesics in the Space of Shells · Heeren, Rumpf, Wardetzky, Wirth (2012)
Foundation for the discrete shell space and elastic metric used in Repulsive Shells.
Discrete Differential Geometry: An Applied Introduction · Crane (2021)
Reference for cotan Laplacian, discrete curvatures, and other mesh-level building blocks.
