01 — Accomplishments

What's working

Two bodies of work are complete and locked in by regression tests (67 checks total, all green): mesh infrastructure with a finite-difference gradient checker, and the full discrete tangent-point energy with Barnes-Hut acceleration, including the gradient. Put together, we can flow a mesh along $-\nabla \hat\Phi$ at $O(n \log n)$ cost per iteration.

Mesh data structure and OpenMesh bridge

Triangle meshes live in a struct-of-arrays layout ($V$ as an $n_v \times 3$ matrix, $F$ as $n_f \times 3$, with optional vertex normals and a cached target edge length $L_0$). A bridge round-trips into an OpenMesh half-edge representation, which later remeshing steps will need for topology-aware edge-split and Delaunay-flip operations. A normalize() routine centers the mesh on its vertex centroid and rescales so the bounding-box diagonal is exactly one, making energy values comparable across inputs of different scales.

Finite-difference gradient checker

Central differences with an adaptive step $h = \epsilon \,(1 + |x_i|)$, calibrated once against a known analytic quadratic. Every analytical gradient we write has to pass this check before any downstream code is allowed to depend on it. The gate caught three sign errors during development that would otherwise have shown up as stalled descent in the gradient-flow demo much later, with no obvious culprit.

Procedural test meshes

Deterministic factories for icosphere (any subdivision level, analytic outward normals, $10 \cdot 4^k + 2$ vertices) and torus (parametric, with outward normals and exact face counts). Every regression test builds its own mesh from these factories rather than loading a file, so nothing on disk can silently drift between runs.

Discrete tangent-point energy and its gradient

A brute-force implementation of $\hat\Phi(x)$ and $\nabla \hat\Phi(x)$ at $\alpha = 6$ via midpoint quadrature over ordered triangle pairs. The gradient propagates through per-face Jacobians for centroid, unit normal, and area with respect to each incident vertex. Every Jacobian is derived in closed form and verified against finite differences on a torus mesh before the acceleration layer goes on top.

Hierarchical acceleration

A top-down SAH bounding volume hierarchy over face AABBs caches four aggregates at every internal node: total area, area-weighted centroid, unweighted normal-sum, and cluster radius. A block-cluster tree built over BVH$\times$BVH applies the multipole acceptance criterion $\max(r_U, r_V) < \theta \, \|c_U - c_V\|$ at $\theta = 0.5$ to decide which cluster pairs can be approximated by a single 0th-order proxy and which have to be expanded down to their leaves.

Barnes-Hut energy and gradient at $O(n \log n)$

The admissible-pair sum (cluster proxy per pair) plus the exact leaf-leaf sum give energy and gradient at near-linear cost. The gradient side needed one subtlety: the set of admissible pairs itself depends on $x$, so we differentiate the cluster-approximated kernel at each fixed admissible block and chain-rule through the aggregate definitions to vertex positions, rather than trying to differentiate the piecewise-varying block assignment itself.

02 — Preliminary results

What we can measure now

Regression suite: 67 checks, all green

test_phase0 runs 20 checks covering the OpenMesh round-trip, the normalization and $L_0$ cache, the gradient checker on a known quadratic, and the test- mesh factories. test_phase1 adds 47 more: FD gradient on face geometry and on brute-force TPE, scale covariance $\hat\Phi(\lambda x) = \lambda^{4-\alpha} \hat\Phi(x)$ (zero relative error, matching the closed- form scaling), translation invariance, cluster-aggregate conservation inside the BVH, exact exhaustive pair coverage in the block-cluster tree, and Barnes-Hut versus brute-force cross-checks.

Barnes-Hut accuracy

At $\theta = 0$ the Barnes-Hut energy matches brute force to $\sim\!3 \times 10^{-15}$ relative error on a 192-face torus (pure floating-point summation-order noise). At $\theta = 0.5$ on icosphere$(3)$ (1280 faces) the relative error is $1.0 \times 10^{-3}$, matching the accuracy claim in the Repulsive Surfaces paper. A $\theta$-sweep on a thin torus gives the expected monotone convergence: $\theta = 0.8 \to 6.9\%$, $\theta = 0.5 \to 4.4\%$, $\theta = 0.1 \to 1.1 \times 10^{-4}$.

$L^2$ gradient flow demo

We descend $\hat\Phi$ with Armijo-backtracked $L^2$ gradient descent on an ellipsoid built by scaling icosphere$(2)$ along its principal axes by $(1.7, 0.75, 0.75)$. The uniform-radial mode is projected out of the gradient each step so descent moves shape rather than size: TPE is scale-covariant at exponent $\lambda^{-2}$, so raw descent would otherwise inflate the mesh indefinitely to lower the energy. The figure below records 95 iterations of the resulting flow.

Figure. $L^2$ gradient flow on the ellipsoid, 95 iterations. Energy drops several-fold then stalls above the round-sphere minimum, and the Armijo step size $\tau$ collapses toward $10^{-12}$ even though $\|\nabla\hat\Phi\|$ stays in the range $10^{1}$–$10^{3}$. This is the expected $L^2$ failure mode (Yu et al. Fig. 5): the per-vertex descent step, being mesh-resolution dependent, can't make progress against the TPE's high-frequency stiffness at fine mesh scales. The $H^s$ preconditioner in the next phase cures it.
03 — Reflection

What worked, and one addition

Every analytical derivative we wrote went through a mandatory finite-difference gate before anything downstream depended on it. At $\alpha = 6$ the TPE kernel has extreme stiffness at short range, so a sign error in the chain rule silently stalls descent rather than throwing; the gate caught three such errors during development, each of which would have manifested much later as a mangled gradient-flow demo with no obvious culprit. Carrying this discipline into the rest of the pipeline is the single most important thing we can take from the work so far.

One addition to the plan

Our FD checks prove internal consistency (the analytical gradient matches the finite difference), not correctness (the kernel itself is right). A bug that flips a sign in both energy and gradient consistently is undetectable by finite differences. Before the optimization work starts, we will cross- validate against the reference CPU implementation (HenrikSchumacher/Repulsor) on a handful of meshes, running the same OBJs through both pipelines and comparing $\hat\Phi$ and $\nabla\hat\Phi$ to the Barnes-Hut tolerance at matching $\theta$. Repulsor is loaded as an external checkout, not vendored. This was not in the original proposal; it replaces "more genus-gallery meshes" as our first correctness check before optimization results go into the write-up.

One small slip

Higher-genus test meshes (genus $\geq 2$ closed surfaces) aren't cleanly available in any open mesh repository we could find. We will add a make_n_torus(g, …) procedural generator when the canonical-embedding gallery needs it, rather than committing a folder of hand-crafted OBJs now.

04 — Updated plan

What's left before May 4

The final is due in two weeks, on May 4. The critical path to a respectable submission is four items, roughly in the order we will tackle them:

$H^s$-preconditioned descent

Cotan Laplacian assembly, brute-force fractional operators $B$ and $B_0$, hierarchical compression for the $L^\sigma$ matvec, and preconditioned GMRES with barycenter constraint via Schur complement. This is the biggest single item remaining and the one on our risk list below; once it's in, the $L^2$ stall in the figure above should disappear and canonical minimizers become reachable.

Cross-validate TPE against Repulsor

Run the same OBJ meshes through our pipeline and through the reference CPU implementation, comparing $\hat\Phi$ and $\nabla\hat\Phi$ to the Barnes-Hut tolerance at matching $\theta$. Turns an internal- consistency claim into a real correctness check before any optimization results go into the write-up.

Canonical embedding gallery

Sphere (genus 0), torus of revolution (genus 1), and as many higher-genus minimizers as the preconditioner handles cleanly. The make_n_torus(g, …) procedural generator gets written here; reproducing the tetrahedral genus-3 minimizer from the Repulsive Surfaces gallery is the "wow moment" we're aiming for.

GPU port of Barnes-Hut

The admissible-pair plus leaf-leaf sum is the single biggest CPU bottleneck per Repulsive Shells Fig. 33, and it's embarrassingly parallel at the pair level. The port begins in parallel with the optimization work, using the stabilized CPU pipeline as the ground-truth reference.

Stretch, if the critical path stays clean. Dynamic remeshing (edge split/collapse, Delaunay flip, tangential smoothing), the membrane-plus- bending shell energy, and the ball-through-tube extrapolation demo (Repulsive Shells Fig. 4). The trust-region path-energy solver for cloth eversion is unlikely in the remaining window and would be post-deadline work.

Risk. The hierarchical-compression step for the $L^\sigma$ matvec is the single trickiest piece of the whole pipeline; any bug silently tanks the preconditioned GMRES iteration count without throwing. We plan to gate it the same way we gated the Barnes-Hut gradient: a $\theta = 0$ fast-versus-brute check to machine precision, and a $\theta = 0.5$ check below 1% relative error, before any downstream code uses it.
